Online coaching for movement, pain relief and mobility: who is succeeding, how they earn, and where there is room for a new brand

A look at the businesses worldwide and in Greece that help people move better and get out of pain. The report describes what these businesses do and how they make money. The final section is the only place where we give our own view on where the best opportunity lies.

📅 19/06/2026 🔎 About 22 brands studied 🔗 Sources linked throughout

iWhat this report covers

This report looks at businesses around the world that help people move better, get out of pain, and improve their mobility — for example easing a sore lower back, stiff hips, or painful knees, and helping people keep moving well as they get older. These businesses are almost always built around one coach or one named training method, and they sell their help online.

We studied about twenty of them: the best-known international names, and the main players in the Greek market. For each one we looked at four simple things — what they sell, how much they charge, how they make their money, and what seems to make them work.

The first four sections simply describe what we found, with no opinions. The final section is the only place where we give our own view: based on the competition and the empty spaces we see, where there is room for a new brand.

1Who is succeeding

The field is led by a small number of well-known coaches and methods. Most of them are based in the United States and work in English, and a few have grown into large companies.

The clearest example of a complete business is Liebscher & Bracht in Germany. They give away a large library of short pain-relief videos on YouTube, which brought them more than 1.5 million followers. That free audience then buys a paid app, books printed in about ten languages, and treatments from a network of more than 3,000 certified therapists. The company is reported to earn around €15 million a year.[1] It matters because it is German, not American — it shows this kind of business can succeed outside the English-speaking world.

Other strong international names work in similar ways. ATG / Knees Over Toes sells an app to the public and certifies other coaches who pay a monthly fee and do the hands-on coaching, so the founder does not coach people himself. The Ready State sells a low monthly subscription with mobility and pain routines organised by body part. Functional Patterns uses a fairly cheap program as the entry point to an expensive coach certification. Three accounts that are often pointed to as comparable — David Grey Rehab, Integrated Kinetic Neurology, and Swolesam — each do well, but each one only uses a single way of earning and leaves the others untouched.

The Greek market looks very different. The coaches with the largest followings focus on general fitness and body shape, not on pain or corrective work. Personal coaching is sold mostly through messaging apps such as Viber and Instagram, with no dedicated app behind it. The fitness certifications on offer carry foreign brands and are simply resold in Greek. In other words, Greece has the customers and the demand, but no recognised home-grown brand built around pain relief, mobility, and a coach's own method.

The pattern among the most successful businesses is clear: they combine these ways into one ladder. They start people with free content, move them to a low-cost program, then to a monthly subscription, and finally offer higher-priced personal coaching and, later, a coach certification. The cheap products bring people in; the subscription gives steady income; and the certification and coach network are what let the business grow without using more of the founder's own time.

3What makes them succeed

Looking across all the successful brands, the same habits come up again and again. These are not opinions; they are the things the winners actually do.

  1. They lead with a clear promise of a result. They sell "get rid of your back pain" or "move well again," and they keep the name of their method in the background as the engine behind that promise, rather than as the headline.
  2. They give away a lot of free teaching content. On YouTube and Instagram they explain why the body hurts and how it works. This free content both attracts customers and lets people see what the paid product is like before they buy.
  3. They let people buy directly from a website. Customers can pay and start on their own, without sending messages back and forth. This keeps the coach's time free and still makes the sale.
  4. They make a separate page for each problem. A page for back pain, another for knees, another for the neck. When someone searches online for that exact problem, they find the brand.
  5. They turn programs into group challenges. Far more people finish a program when they do it together as a timed group than when they do it alone — and people who finish get results, leave good reviews, and stay longer.
  6. They let other coaches do the hands-on work. The founder owns the method and the brand, while certified coaches deliver the coaching. This is how the business grows without the founder working more hours.
  7. They use one cheap product to feed two income streams. The same low-cost program that the public buys is also the first step a coach must complete before getting certified, so one piece of content does two jobs.

4How many customers stay

A monthly subscription only works if people keep paying month after month, and keeping them is the hardest part of the whole business. It is also the reason group challenges and communities matter so much. The figures below come from several independent industry sources and describe the field as a whole.[2]

The simple message is this: selling a subscription is not "money for nothing." Customers leave steadily, so the business must keep producing content and, above all, must use group challenges and a community to keep people active. Those two things are the difference between a subscription that slowly empties and one that grows.

5Where the opportunity is

This is the one section where we give our own view. Everything above was a description of the market; what follows is our reading of where there is room for a new brand.

How big the audience could be

A Greek-language product is not limited to the ten million people in Greece. There are roughly five million more people of Greek origin living abroad (estimates range from three to seven million, depending on how you count), most of them in the United States, Australia, and Germany — all wealthy countries.[3] These communities still feel a strong connection to Greek language and culture, which makes them easier to reach and quicker to trust than a cold English-speaking audience. A new brand can serve this worldwide Greek community first, where there is almost no competition, and then add English content to reach everyone else. This is exactly the path Liebscher & Bracht followed: first in German, and only later in English.[1]

The most promising path

Based on what works in this market, the most promising plan is to build the ladder of offers described earlier, in a sensible order:

  1. Start with one clear program that solves a specific, common problem. A sore lower back is the strongest candidate, because so many people search for help with it. Price it above the cheap general libraries (which sit at €10–15 a month), because it is specific and gives a real result.
  2. Turn it into a monthly subscription with a community and group challenges. This is the steady income, and the community and challenges are what keep people paying — the single most important factor for success in this market.
  3. Keep a small number of higher-priced personal-coaching places. These bring in good money and strong success stories, but they should stay small, because personal coaching cannot grow without more hours.
  4. Later, teach and certify other coaches, and build a network of them. Once the method is proven and demand is clear, this is the step that lets income grow without more of the founder's time — and it is the part that almost no one in the Greek market offers today.
The gap, in one sentence

The open space is a Greek-language brand for movement, pain relief and mobility — especially for people over 40 — built around its own named method and a recognisable coach, that begins with the worldwide Greek community and later expands into English. No brand holds that position today.

One advantage we already hold

Most of the brands above had to build their own app and software over several years before they could grow. We already have a ready-made coaching platform and app. That means the subscription, the personal coaching, and the coach network can all be delivered in a proper, branded app from day one, instead of being built from scratch — which is a real head start in a market where the app and the community are what keep customers paying.
